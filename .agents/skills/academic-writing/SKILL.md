# Academic Writing Skill

Use this skill when drafting or revising the English manuscript.

## Objective

Produce publication-appropriate academic English for a survey paper. The goal is not literary flourish. The goal is clear, precise, technically dense exposition with proper hedging and strong synthesis.

## Core rules

- Write in formal academic English.
- Prefer synthesis over paper-by-paper cataloguing.
- Every paragraph should have a purpose and a coherent argument.
- Distinguish established evidence from emerging claims.
- Use cautious language where evidence is limited.
- Avoid inflated novelty rhetoric.
- Avoid vague filler such as "many studies have shown" unless specific citations and synthesis follow.
- Do not cite papers unless the evidence cards support the paragraph's claim.

## Paragraph design

For most body paragraphs, aim for this structure:

1. topic sentence that states the conceptual point;
2. comparative or explanatory synthesis across cited papers;
3. technical detail on why the cited papers differ or matter;
4. limitation, caveat, or unresolved issue when relevant.

## Section expectations

- Abstract: scope, method, key findings, open problems.
- Introduction: why the topic matters, what the survey covers, and how it is organized.
- Methods/Search Strategy: how papers were gathered and screened, including Paperclip and web-search integration.
- Core sections: taxonomy or conceptual grouping, with technically meaningful comparison.
- Limitations: corpus limits, search limits, evidence quality, publication-status issues.
- Conclusion: concise synthesis and forward-looking problems.

## Style expectations

- Prefer concise technical statements over conversational phrases.
- Define jargon when it first appears if the field is broad.
- When discussing model classes, tasks, or benchmarks, specify what differentiates them.
- Use transition language to explain relationships between concepts.
- Keep tables and figures aligned with the prose rather than duplicating them mechanically.
